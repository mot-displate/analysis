01
Raising the LE Threshold from €120 to €130 — What Changes
The previous threshold of AOV > €120 was catching 177 days as LE (43% of the dataset). At €130, only 24 days qualify (6%) — days with truly exceptional basket sizes from LE drops. This is the more defensible cutoff and gives us a much larger non-LE analysis pool.

The €130 cutoff is justified: TOV per day jumps sharply above €130 AOV — that's when LE products are driving large basket sizes. Below €130, AOV variability is normal promo mix; above €130 it's LE-driven. The previous €120 threshold was discarding many ordinary promo days as false-positives.
02
Two Discount Signals: Entry Price vs Multi-Plate Incentive
Every progressive promo has two rates: the 1-plate rate (entry price, affects demand breadth) and the max rate (3+ plates, affects basket depth). They move together but are different levers. The spread between them averages 6.7pp and represents how hard the business is pushing volume-buying.
1-plate discount — entry price signal
The rate a single-plate buyer sees. Drives conversion breadth — whether a casual visitor buys at all. Most sensitive to price-conscious customers. Range in dataset: 0–33%, mean 20.6%. Simple correlation with TOV: r = +0.36.
Max discount — multi-plate incentive signal
The rate a 3+ plate buyer sees. Drives basket depth — whether an existing buyer adds more to their order. Correlates more strongly with measured DR (r = 0.68 vs 0.62 for 1-plate). Range: 0–40%, mean 27.3%. Simple correlation with TOV: r = +0.32.

Both signals show the same monotonic pattern: deeper discount → higher avg TOV. The max discount gradient is smoother (no outlier at the top) because it captures basket depth which is more broadly distributed. Neither signal shows a cliff — there's no evidence of a threshold below which discounts fail to lift TOV.
Progressive spread distribution — how many days have tiered vs flat promos
98 flat days (spread = 0): same rate for all plates. 272 progressive days with a spread of 5–20pp — the business consistently uses tiered pricing to incentivise multi-plate purchases. The spread itself has r = +0.09 with TOV (p = 0.09) — barely significant, suggesting the structure of the incentive (tiered vs flat) matters less than the depth.
03
The Model: Discount Depth Still Has No Independent Effect on TOV
Three model variants — using disc_1 alone, disc_max alone, and both together — all reach the same conclusion: once AcqSpend and CIR are controlled, neither discount signal has a statistically significant partial effect on TOV. The model R² of 95.9% is explained almost entirely by spend and efficiency.

04
Jan–Mar 2026 vs 2025: Spend Drove the Decline, Not Discount Depth
With the corrected LE threshold, the picture sharpens. Discount depth rose modestly on both metrics (+1–7pp). Spend fell 14–23% in every month. TOV fell 4–8%. The March story is notable: max discount rose +3.4pp (the biggest disc_max move) but TOV fell the most (−8.2%). More discount, less revenue.

05
Four-Year Escalation: Both Signals Have Drifted Up, But Slowly
Examining the full 2023–2026 promo calendar (ex Nov–Dec), discount depth on both metrics has increased gradually — but the trajectory is slow, not alarming. The bigger story is that the spread between d1 and dmax is stable at 6–7pp, meaning the structure of the incentive hasn't changed even as depth crept up.

06
Revised Conclusions
Six findings from the corrected analysis. Two are materially updated from v1/v2; four are confirmed with higher confidence on a larger dataset.
Confirmed · stronger
Neither discount signal independently drives TOV
Tested across three model specifications on 370 non-LE days (vs 217 before). disc_1 partial r = +0.05 (p=0.38), disc_max partial r = −0.01 (p=0.83). Using both together adds nothing: each partial r stays near zero and they partially offset each other. R² = 95.9% comes from AcqSpend (+0.97) and CIR (−0.97) alone. Larger sample, same verdict.
Updated
Max discount correlates more strongly with measured DR — that's the basket mix story
disc_max → measured DR: r = 0.68. disc_1 → measured DR: r = 0.62. The max rate explains more of the DR variation because it directly sets what multi-plate buyers (who dominate order volume) pay. The 6.7pp average spread between d1 and dmax means the measured DR will always sit between these two anchors, weighted by basket composition.
Updated · sharpened
March 2026 is the definitive test — and discounts failed it
disc_1 barely moved (+0.2pp). disc_max rose +3.4pp — the largest single-month increase in the dataset. Spend fell −14.4%. TOV fell −8.2% — the worst YoY month in Jan–Mar. If the max discount had any compensating effect on basket depth and TOV, March was its chance. The data shows zero compensation. Deeper multi-plate discounts did not offset lower spend.
Confirmed
Both signals show raw TOV correlation — but it's a confound
In the raw data: d1 r=+0.36, dmax r=+0.32 with TOV. Both real, both significant. But higher-discount days also carry higher AcqSpend (€84k → €105k from low to high disc_max bracket) and better CIR. Once those are held constant, the discount signal evaporates. The discount is tagging a high-investment day, not causing the revenue.
Nuance
Discount escalation is real but slow — not a spiral
d1: +2.1pp over four years. dmax: +2.7pp. Spread: stable at 6–7pp. Free-shipping days declining (18 in 2023 → 8 in 2026). The structure of the incentive is unchanged. This is drift, not dependency. The more important question is whether the same discount depth is delivering the same demand response — or whether customers have been trained to expect it and AOV is now the ceiling without further escalation.
Confirmed
The right question is spend reinvestment, not discount restructuring
CIR is at target (36–39% Jan–Mar 2026). The regression says each €1 of AcqSpend generates ~€2.58 in TOV at current efficiency. If the target is ≤40% CIR, there is headroom to reinvest spend and recover the −5.7% YoY TOV gap — without touching discount depth. Increasing discount depth to try to recover TOV without spend is the wrong lever, and the data confirms it.
